The retinol vs tretinoin question is one of the most common in skincare. Both are vitamin A derivatives. Both promote cell turnover and collagen production. But they differ significantly in potency, irritation profile, accessibility, and evidence base. Here is a practical framework for deciding which is right for you.

## The core difference: conversion steps

[Tretinoin](/ingredients/tretinoin/) (all-trans retinoic acid) is the active form of vitamin A. It binds directly to retinoic acid receptors in the skin. No conversion is needed — it works immediately at the cellular level.

[Retinol](/ingredients/retinol/) must undergo two enzymatic conversion steps in the skin: retinol → retinaldehyde → retinoic acid. Each conversion step involves some loss of potency. This makes retinol inherently weaker than tretinoin, but also less irritating — the slower delivery is a feature, not a bug.

This is why our [Retinol vs Tretinoin comparison](/comparisons/retinol-vs-tretinoin/) shows tretinoin rated as "Strong" evidence (decades of FDA-approved research) while retinol is also "Strong" but with the caveat that it is studied at much higher concentrations and longer timeframes to achieve comparable results.

## When retinol is enough

For most people starting retinoids, retinol is the right entry point. Here is when it makes sense:

**Mild concerns.** If your goals are subtle — improving skin texture, preventing early signs of ageing, maintaining healthy cell turnover — retinol at 0.3–0.5% used consistently provides meaningful benefits over time.

**Sensitive skin.** If you have reactive skin, rosacea tendencies, or a history of irritation with actives, retinol's gentler delivery is an advantage. You can also consider [retinaldehyde](/comparisons/retinal-vs-retinol/) as a middle ground — one conversion step closer to retinoic acid than retinol, potentially faster-acting with similar tolerability.

**No prescriber access.** Retinol is available OTC worldwide. Tretinoin requires a prescription in most countries. If accessing a dermatologist is difficult or expensive, retinol is a practical alternative.

**Combination with other actives.** If your routine includes [glycolic acid](/ingredients/glycolic-acid/) or [vitamin C](/ingredients/vitamin-c/), the lower potency of retinol creates more room for layering without overwhelming your barrier. Check combinations with the [Stack Checker](/stack-checker/).

## When to consider tretinoin

Tretinoin becomes the better choice when retinol's limitations are holding you back:

**Moderate-to-severe photoageing.** If you have visible wrinkles, significant sun damage, or textural changes that retinol has not meaningfully improved after 6+ months, tretinoin's direct receptor activation provides a stronger signal for collagen remodelling.

**Acne treatment.** Tretinoin has been FDA-approved for acne since 1971. While retinol can help mild comedonal acne, tretinoin's potency makes it more effective for persistent breakouts. See our [Acne Protocol](/protocols/acne/) for where it fits.

**You have adapted to retinol.** If you have used retinol consistently for 6+ months, tolerate it well, and want to escalate, tretinoin is the logical next step. Your skin's retinoid tolerance translates — you may experience less retinisation than someone starting tretinoin cold.

**Prescription supervision available.** Tretinoin is best used with dermatologist guidance for concentration selection (0.025%, 0.05%, 0.1%), formulation choice (cream vs gel vs microsphere), and monitoring for irritation.

## Practical tips for both

Regardless of which retinoid you choose, these principles apply:

**Start low, go slow.** Retinol: begin at 0.25–0.3%, 2–3 nights per week. Tretinoin: begin at 0.025%, same frequency. Increase over 2–3 months.

**Apply to dry skin.** Damp skin increases penetration and irritation. Wait 10–20 minutes after cleansing, or use the sandwich method: moisturiser → retinoid → moisturiser.

**Sunscreen is non-negotiable.** Both retinol and tretinoin increase photosensitivity. Daily SPF 30+ is essential — without it, you risk hyperpigmentation and are undermining the anti-ageing benefits. See our [sunscreen profile](/ingredients/sunscreen/) for guidance.

**Support your barrier.** [Ceramides](/ingredients/ceramides/), [hyaluronic acid](/ingredients/hyaluronic-acid/), and [niacinamide](/ingredients/niacinamide/) help manage the dryness and peeling that retinoids can cause. Build a supportive base routine before introducing retinoids.

**Consider oral support.** [Oral collagen peptides](/supplements/collagen-peptides/) combined with a topical retinoid is a complementary inside-out approach to collagen support. [Omega-3s](/supplements/omega-3/) may also help manage retinoid-related dryness by supporting barrier lipids from within.

## The bottom line

Retinol is where most people should start. It is effective, accessible, and forgiving. Tretinoin is where you go when you need more — more potency, more evidence, and more results, with the trade-off of more irritation and a prescription requirement.

Neither is wasted time. Retinol builds tolerance that prepares your skin for tretinoin if you eventually want to escalate. And for many people, well-formulated retinol at 0.5% used consistently is enough to achieve their goals without ever needing a prescription.
