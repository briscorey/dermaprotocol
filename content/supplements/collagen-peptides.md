category: "Protein / Peptide"
summary: "Hydrolysed collagen peptides (typically 2.5–15g daily) have moderate evidence for improving skin hydration and elasticity, with some data supporting reduced wrinkle depth after 8–12 weeks."
evidence_strength: "moderate"
tags: ["anti-ageing", "hydration", "elasticity"]
what_it_does: |
  Oral collagen peptides are broken down during digestion into smaller peptide fragments (primarily hydroxyproline-containing di- and tripeptides) that are absorbed into the bloodstream. These fragments may stimulate fibroblasts in the dermis to increase endogenous collagen, elastin, and hyaluronic acid production. The mechanism is thought to involve both direct peptide signalling and a "wound repair" response triggered by the presence of collagen fragments.

  Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses of randomised controlled trials show statistically significant improvements in skin hydration, elasticity, and wrinkle depth compared to placebo. However, most individual studies are small (30–120 participants), industry-funded, and use varying formulations, making it difficult to isolate the most effective type, source, or dose.
skin_benefits:
  - "Improved skin hydration (the most consistently supported outcome)"
  - "Increased skin elasticity"
  - "Modest reduction in wrinkle depth (typically shallow wrinkles)"
  - "Possible support for wound healing and nail strength"
dosing: |
  **Effective range:** 2.5–15g daily of hydrolysed collagen peptides. Most positive studies use 2.5–10g.

  **Timing:** No strong evidence for a specific time of day. Some people take it with morning coffee or a meal for consistency and to reduce GI discomfort.

  **Duration:** Most studies show effects emerging at 4–8 weeks, with best results at 8–12 weeks of consistent daily use.

  **Note on molecular weight:** Hydrolysed collagen (molecular weight typically 2,000–6,000 Da) is better absorbed than gelatin or native collagen. Look for products specifying "hydrolysed collagen peptides" rather than just "collagen."
forms: |
  **Preferred:** Hydrolysed collagen peptides (powder or capsule). Marine (fish-derived) and bovine are the most studied sources. Marine collagen is predominantly Type I; bovine includes Types I and III.

  **Avoid:** Native (undenatured) collagen for skin purposes — it is studied primarily for joint health via a different mechanism (immune modulation). Gelatin is poorly standardised for skin outcomes.

  **Type I collagen** is the most relevant for skin, as it constitutes approximately 80% of dermal collagen.
topical_interaction: |
  **Does oral collagen replace topical collagen?** No. Topical collagen has large molecular weight and does not penetrate the epidermis to reach the dermis. It functions only as a surface moisturiser. Oral collagen peptides work through a completely different mechanism — systemic absorption and fibroblast stimulation.

  **Combining with topical retinoids:** This is a sensible combination. Retinoids (tretinoin, retinol) stimulate collagen production from the outside; oral collagen peptides may support it from the inside. No known negative interactions. Some dermatologists recommend this pairing for anti-ageing protocols.

  **Combining with topical vitamin C:** Also complementary. Vitamin C is a cofactor in collagen synthesis. Using a topical vitamin C serum (for localised antioxidant protection) alongside oral collagen peptides addresses collagen support from two different angles.

  **With oral vitamin C:** Ensuring adequate vitamin C intake (dietary or supplemental) supports the enzymatic reactions required for collagen crosslinking. Deficiency impairs collagen synthesis regardless of peptide intake.
caution_profile: "Generally well tolerated. Some people experience mild GI symptoms (bloating, aftertaste). Marine collagen is not suitable for those with fish allergies. Bovine collagen is not suitable for those avoiding animal products. Quality and purity vary significantly between brands — third-party testing (NSF, USP) is a meaningful differentiator."
who_benefits: "Adults over 30 noticing early skin ageing signs (reduced elasticity, dryness, fine lines), especially when combined with a topical retinoid and consistent sunscreen use. Less likely to produce dramatic results for deep wrinkles or severe photoageing."
common_mistakes:
  - "Expecting visible results in under 4 weeks"
  - "Using low-quality collagen without third-party testing"
  - "Confusing native collagen (for joints) with hydrolysed peptides (for skin)"
  - "Relying on collagen alone without addressing topical routine and sun protection"
  - "Using collagen drinks with high sugar content — the added sugar may offset skin benefits"
key_studies: |
  **Systematic review (2021):** A meta-analysis of 19 RCTs (n=1,125) found hydrolysed collagen supplementation significantly improved skin hydration, elasticity, and wrinkles compared to placebo. Effect sizes were moderate. Most studies were 8–12 weeks. (*Journal of Drugs in Dermatology*)

  **Proksch et al. (2014):** 69 women aged 35–55, 2.5g or 5g collagen peptides daily for 8 weeks. Both doses significantly improved skin elasticity versus placebo. Effect was most pronounced in women over 50. (*Skin Pharmacology and Physiology*)

  **Limitation:** Most collagen studies are funded by collagen manufacturers. Independently funded, large-scale RCTs are lacking. Publication bias is a concern — negative results may be underreported.
timeline: "Hydration improvements may be noticeable within 4–6 weeks. Elasticity and wrinkle depth changes typically require 8–12 weeks of consistent daily use. Benefits may diminish after discontinuation."
disclaimer: "Collagen supplements are not a substitute for sun protection, a balanced diet, or professional dermatological care. Evidence is moderate and primarily from industry-funded studies. Individual results vary."
