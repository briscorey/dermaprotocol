description: "An essential mineral for wound healing, immune function, and acne management. Often deficient in acne patients."
category: "Essential Mineral"
summary: "Zinc plays a critical role in skin cell turnover, wound healing, and immune regulation. Oral zinc supplementation has moderate evidence for reducing acne severity, particularly in people with low baseline zinc status."
evidence_strength: "moderate"
tags: ["acne", "wound healing", "immune support"]
what_it_does: |
  Zinc is a cofactor for over 300 enzymes involved in cell division, protein synthesis, immune function, and wound healing. In skin specifically, zinc supports keratinocyte proliferation, modulates inflammatory pathways, has direct antibacterial properties against *Cutibacterium acnes*, and may reduce sebum production through mild anti-androgen activity (5-alpha reductase inhibition).

  Zinc deficiency is associated with impaired wound healing, dermatitis, and increased susceptibility to skin infections. Several studies have found that acne patients tend to have lower serum zinc levels than age-matched controls, suggesting a possible relationship between zinc status and acne severity.
skin_benefits:
  - "Reduction in inflammatory acne lesions (moderate evidence)"
  - "Faster wound healing and tissue repair"
  - "Anti-inflammatory effects relevant to acne and dermatitis"
  - "Mild antibacterial activity against acne-causing bacteria"
  - "Possible sebum regulation through anti-androgen effects"
dosing: |
  **Effective range for acne:** 30–50mg elemental zinc daily, typically for 8–12 weeks. Studies showing benefit most commonly use 30mg elemental zinc.

  **Upper limit:** The tolerable upper intake level is 40mg/day for adults (including dietary intake). Doses above this may be used therapeutically short-term but carry higher risk of side effects, particularly copper depletion.

  **Timing:** Take with food to minimise nausea. Do not take at the same time as iron, calcium, or phytate-rich foods, as these inhibit zinc absorption.

  **Copper consideration:** Chronic zinc supplementation above 30mg/day can deplete copper over time. If supplementing zinc for more than 8 weeks, consider a supplement that includes 1–2mg copper, or monitor copper status.
forms: |
  **Preferred for bioavailability:** Zinc picolinate and zinc bisglycinate (chelated forms) have the best absorption data. Zinc gluconate is also well-absorbed and widely available.

  **Acceptable:** Zinc citrate, zinc acetate. These are reasonably well-absorbed.

  **Less ideal:** Zinc oxide and zinc sulfate have lower bioavailability. Zinc sulfate was used in many older acne studies, however, and still showed benefit — it just causes more GI side effects.

  **Elemental zinc content matters:** Check the label for elemental zinc, not total compound weight. 50mg zinc gluconate contains approximately 7mg elemental zinc. 30mg zinc picolinate contains approximately 6mg elemental zinc.
topical_interaction: |
  **Oral zinc and topical zinc oxide (sunscreen):** These serve completely different functions. Oral zinc provides systemic immune and anti-inflammatory support. Topical zinc oxide in mineral sunscreens provides UV protection through physical light scattering. They are complementary, not redundant.

  **Oral zinc and topical retinoids:** Zinc supports wound healing and may reduce retinoid-induced irritation by supporting barrier recovery. Some dermatologists recommend zinc supplementation alongside retinoid introduction, though this is based on clinical experience rather than controlled trials.

  **Oral zinc and topical niacinamide:** Both reduce inflammation through different pathways (zinc systemically, niacinamide locally). This is a sensible combination for acne protocols — no known negative interactions.

  **Oral zinc and topical benzoyl peroxide:** Both have antibacterial properties against *C. acnes*. Using both may provide additive bacterial reduction. No interaction concerns.

  **Oral zinc and oral antibiotics for acne:** Some studies suggest oral zinc may be an alternative to antibiotics for mild-moderate inflammatory acne, avoiding antibiotic resistance concerns. Discuss with your dermatologist if considering this substitution.
caution_profile: "Nausea is the most common side effect, especially on an empty stomach or with zinc sulfate. Long-term supplementation above 30mg/day may deplete copper (symptoms include fatigue, anaemia, neurological changes). Do not exceed 40mg elemental zinc daily without medical supervision. Zinc can interact with certain antibiotics (tetracyclines, quinolones) — separate by at least 2 hours."
who_benefits: "People with inflammatory acne (especially if dietary zinc intake is low), those with slow wound healing, and anyone with confirmed or suspected zinc deficiency. Vegetarians and vegans are at higher risk of low zinc status due to phytate-rich diets reducing absorption."
common_mistakes:
  - "Not checking elemental zinc content on the label"
  - "Taking zinc on an empty stomach (causes nausea)"
  - "Taking zinc at the same time as iron or calcium supplements"
  - "Long-term high-dose supplementation without monitoring copper"
  - "Expecting zinc to work as fast as topical treatments — systemic effects take weeks"
key_studies: |
  **Yee et al. (2020):** Meta-analysis of 10 studies found serum zinc levels were significantly lower in acne patients compared to controls. Zinc supplementation was associated with clinical improvement, particularly in inflammatory acne. (*Dermatologic Therapy*)

  **Dreno et al. (2001):** 332 patients with inflammatory acne, 30mg zinc gluconate daily vs placebo for 3 months. Zinc group showed significant reduction in inflammatory lesion count (31.2% improvement vs placebo). (*Acta Dermato-Venereologica*)

  **Brandt (2013):** Review of zinc's role in skin biology — wound healing, immune function, UV protection, and anti-inflammatory effects. Concluded that zinc deficiency is underdiagnosed and may contribute to various dermatological conditions. (*Journal of Drugs in Dermatology*)
timeline: "Initial effects on inflammation may begin at 4–6 weeks. Acne studies typically show meaningful improvement at 8–12 weeks. Wound healing support is more immediate if deficiency is corrected."
