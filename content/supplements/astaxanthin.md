what_it_does: |
  Astaxanthin is a naturally occurring carotenoid pigment (responsible for the red colour of salmon, shrimp, and microalgae) with antioxidant potency estimated at 10–100x greater than beta-carotene and 500x greater than vitamin E in certain in vitro assays. Unlike some antioxidants, astaxanthin can span the full width of a cell membrane, providing protection against both lipid peroxidation (inside the membrane) and free radicals in the aqueous environment.

  For skin, astaxanthin accumulates in dermal tissues after oral ingestion and may reduce UV-induced oxidative stress, inhibit MMP expression (enzymes that break down collagen), and modulate inflammatory cytokines. Several small RCTs show positive outcomes, but the evidence base remains limited by study size, industry funding, and short durations.

dosing: |
  **Studied range:** 4–12mg daily. Most positive skin studies use 4–6mg daily.

  **Timing:** Take with a fat-containing meal — astaxanthin is lipophilic and absorption depends on dietary fat.

  **Duration:** Studies showing skin benefits are typically 6–16 weeks.

  **Safety margin:** Doses up to 40mg daily have been used in short-term studies without significant adverse effects, though routine supplementation at this level is unnecessary.
forms: |
  **Preferred:** Natural astaxanthin derived from *Haematococcus pluvialis* microalgae. This is the form used in virtually all clinical studies.

  **Avoid:** Synthetic astaxanthin is primarily used in aquaculture feed and has different stereoisomer profiles than natural forms. Its efficacy and safety for human supplementation is less studied.

  **Format:** Softgels in oil are standard. The lipid matrix aids absorption.
topical_interaction: |
  **Oral astaxanthin and topical vitamin C:** Both provide antioxidant protection but through different mechanisms and in different tissue compartments. Oral astaxanthin works systemically (accumulating in dermal tissue), while topical vitamin C provides concentrated local antioxidant defence in the epidermis. This combination addresses oxidative stress from both inside and outside.

  **With sunscreen:** Astaxanthin does not replace sunscreen. Some studies suggest it may modestly reduce UV-induced erythema (similar to omega-3s), but this is supplementary protection only. Think of it as raising systemic antioxidant defence against the UV damage that inevitably penetrates sunscreen.

  **With topical retinoids:** Retinoids increase cell turnover and can increase oxidative stress during the retinisation period. Systemic antioxidant support from astaxanthin may be theoretically beneficial during retinoid introduction, though this specific combination has not been studied.

  **Skin colour note:** At higher doses (12mg+), astaxanthin can produce a subtle warm skin tint due to carotenoid deposition. This is harmless but noticeable in some people and may affect the appearance of topical products.
caution_profile: "Generally well tolerated. May cause a subtle reddish/warm skin tint at higher doses (harmless). People with carotenoid sensitivities or allergies to seafood/algae should use caution."

key_studies: |
  **Tominaga et al. (2012):** 36 men, 6mg astaxanthin daily for 6 weeks. Significant improvements in wrinkle depth, elasticity, and moisture content versus placebo. Small study. (*Journal of Clinical Biochemistry and Nutrition*)

  **Ito et al. (2018):** Systematic review of 11 RCTs concluded that astaxanthin supplementation improved skin wrinkles, elasticity, and moisture, with the most consistent effects on wrinkle parameters. Noted that most studies were small and industry-funded. (*Marine Drugs*)

  **Camera et al. (2009):** In vitro and ex vivo data showing astaxanthin accumulates in human skin tissue after oral intake and reduces UV-induced oxidative markers. Supports the biological plausibility of clinical findings. (*Experimental Dermatology*)
