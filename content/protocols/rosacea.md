<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Who This Is For</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">
<p>This protocol targets people with mild to moderate rosacea — persistent facial redness, visible blood vessels, and occasional inflammatory papules/pustules. It focuses on the subtype 1 (erythematotelangiectatic) and subtype 2 (papulopustular) presentations that are most responsive to topical management.</p>
<p>Severe rosacea, ocular rosacea, or rhinophyma (thickened skin, usually on the nose) require professional dermatological care and often prescription treatments beyond what OTC products can address.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Core Principles</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">

<div class="alert alert--tip" style="margin-bottom:var(--sp-4)">
<div class="alert__title">The rosacea paradox</div>
Rosacea skin is simultaneously inflamed and fragile. Most acne and anti-ageing actives will make it worse. The first rule is do no harm — simplify radically, eliminate triggers, and introduce only the few actives with evidence for rosacea specifically.
</div>

<p><strong>Key differences from other skin concerns:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Most exfoliating acids (AHA, BHA) are poorly tolerated and should be avoided initially</li>
<li>Retinoids are often too irritating — if introduced at all, it must be extremely slowly</li>
<li>Benzoyl peroxide is generally too harsh for rosacea-prone skin</li>
<li>Fragrance, essential oils, and alcohol-based products are common triggers</li>
<li>Heat, spicy food, alcohol, and intense exercise can trigger flares — these are management factors, not product choices</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Optional Additions (proceed with extreme caution)</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">
<ul>
<li><strong>Centella asiatica / madecassoside</strong> — One of the few botanicals with genuine calming evidence. Can be layered under moisturiser.</li>
<li><strong>Prescription metronidazole 0.75–1%</strong> — First-line prescription for papulopustular rosacea. Requires a dermatologist.</li>
<li><strong>Prescription ivermectin 1% (Soolantra)</strong> — Effective for papulopustular rosacea, particularly when Demodex mites are a factor. Requires prescription.</li>
<li><strong>Prescription brimonidine (Mirvaso) / oxymetazoline (Rhofade)</strong> — For persistent background redness. Prescription only. Can cause rebound redness — discuss risks with your dermatologist.</li>
</ul>
</div>

<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Trigger Management</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">
<p>Topical management is only half the picture. Common rosacea triggers include:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Heat</strong> — Hot showers, saunas, hot beverages (let coffee cool slightly)</li>
<li><strong>UV exposure</strong> — The most consistent trigger. Mineral sunscreen daily, no exceptions.</li>
<li><strong>Spicy food</strong> — Capsaicin is a vasodilator. If you notice flushing after spicy meals, reduce intake.</li>
<li><strong>Alcohol</strong> — Red wine is the most commonly reported trigger, but any alcohol can cause vasodilation.</li>
<li><strong>Intense exercise</strong> — Counterintuitively, exercise is still recommended for overall health. Cool down strategies help: exercise in cooler environments, use a cool towel, and splash cold water on your face post-workout.</li>
<li><strong>Emotional stress</strong> — Hard to avoid, but mindfulness and stress management have measurable effects on rosacea flares.</li>
</ul>
<p>Keep a trigger diary for 4 weeks to identify your personal pattern. Not all triggers affect everyone equally.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Common Mistakes</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">
<ul>
<li>Treating rosacea like acne — most acne products will make rosacea worse</li>
<li>Using AHA/BHA exfoliants on rosacea-prone skin (too irritating for most)</li>
<li>Applying products to damp or warm skin (increases absorption and irritation)</li>
<li>Using chemical sunscreens that sting reactive skin — switch to zinc oxide</li>
<li>Not giving the minimal routine enough time before adding actives</li>
<li>Ignoring lifestyle triggers while focusing only on products</li>
</ul>
</div>

<div class="detail-section__body">
<p><strong>Weeks 1–2:</strong> Simplified routine reduces new irritation. Skin may start feeling less reactive.</p>
<p><strong>Weeks 4–6:</strong> Azelaic acid begins reducing papules and background inflammation. Redness may modestly improve.</p>

<p><strong>Ongoing:</strong> Rosacea is a chronic condition. Management is ongoing, not curative. The goal is sustained control with minimal products.</p>

<div class="alert alert--caution" style="margin-top:var(--sp-8)">
<div class="alert__title">When to see a dermatologist</div>
If OTC management does not meaningfully improve your rosacea within 12 weeks, if you are experiencing ocular symptoms (dry, gritty, burning eyes), or if papules and pustules are persistent, consult a dermatologist. Prescription options (metronidazole, ivermectin, brimonidine, low-dose doxycycline) are often necessary for adequate control.
