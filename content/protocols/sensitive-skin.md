<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Who This Is For</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">
<p>This protocol is for people whose skin regularly reacts to products that most people tolerate — stinging, burning, redness, tightness, or flushing with cleansers, moisturisers, or sunscreens. Sensitive skin can be a baseline skin type or the result of barrier damage from over-exfoliation, retinoid overuse, or environmental stress.</p>
<p><strong>Important distinction:</strong> If your skin is newly sensitive after introducing actives, see the <a href="/protocols/barrier-repair/">Barrier Repair Protocol</a> first. That protocol focuses on recovery. This one is for people who are chronically reactive and need a long-term strategy.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Core Principles</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">

<div class="alert alert--tip" style="margin-bottom:var(--sp-4)">
<div class="alert__title">The golden rule of sensitive skin</div>
Fewer ingredients per product. Fewer products per routine. Fewer changes per month. Patience is the most important active in your routine.
</div>

<ul>
<li><strong>Fragrance-free everything.</strong> Fragrance (including "natural" essential oils like lavender, tea tree, eucalyptus) is the most common cause of cosmetic contact dermatitis.</li>
<li><strong>Short ingredient lists.</strong> More ingredients means more potential triggers. Choose products with 10–15 ingredients over those with 30+.</li>
<li><strong>Patch test every new product.</strong> Apply a small amount behind your ear or on your inner forearm for 48 hours before facial use.</li>
<li><strong>One new product at a time.</strong> Wait 2 weeks between introductions so you can identify the cause if a reaction occurs.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<h2 class="detail-section__title">Core Protocol</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">

<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">When and How to Introduce Actives</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">
<p>Only consider adding actives after your baseline routine has been comfortable for at least 4 weeks with no stinging, burning, or redness. Then introduce one at a time, starting with the gentlest options:</p>

<p><strong>Tier 1 — Safest first introductions:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Niacinamide 4–5%</strong> — The gentlest effective active. Barrier support, mild oil control, anti-inflammatory. Start 3x per week.</li>
<li><strong>Centella asiatica (cica)</strong> — Calming, wound-healing support. Very well tolerated.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Tier 2 — After 8+ weeks of stability:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Azelaic acid 10%</strong> — The most tolerable active for pigmentation and acne. Start 2x per week on dry skin.</li>
<li><strong>Alpha arbutin 1–2%</strong> — Gentle brightening with minimal irritation risk.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Tier 3 — Approach with caution (12+ weeks of stability):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Retinal (retinaldehyde) 0.025–0.05%</strong> — If you want retinoid benefits, retinal may be better tolerated than retinol. Start once per week. The sandwich method (moisturiser → retinal → moisturiser) reduces irritation.</li>
<li><strong>Mandelic acid 5%</strong> — The gentlest AHA. Larger molecular size means slower, less irritating penetration. Once per week maximum initially.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Supplement Support</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">
<ul>
<li><strong><a href="/supplements/omega-3/">Omega-3 (1–2g EPA+DHA daily)</a></strong> — Supports skin barrier lipid composition from the inside. Addresses systemic inflammation that contributes to reactivity.</li>
<li><strong><a href="/supplements/vitamin-d/">Vitamin D (1,000–2,000 IU daily)</a></strong> — Supports keratinocyte differentiation and barrier formation. Particularly important if you wear daily sunscreen.</li>
<li><strong><a href="/supplements/magnesium/">Magnesium glycinate (200–400mg before bed)</a></strong> — Sleep quality directly affects barrier recovery. Stress reduction is a meaningful skin intervention for sensitive types.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Ingredients to Avoid</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">
<ul>
<li><strong>Fragrance</strong> (including essential oils — lavender, tea tree, peppermint, citrus oils)</li>
<li><strong>Denatured alcohol</strong> (alcohol denat., SD alcohol) in high amounts</li>
<li><strong>Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS)</strong> — a harsh surfactant found in many foaming cleansers</li>
<li><strong>High-concentration actives</strong> — avoid 10%+ niacinamide, 15%+ vitamin C, high-strength AHAs</li>
<li><strong>Physical scrubs</strong> — microbeads, walnut shell, sugar scrubs. Mechanical exfoliation is too aggressive.</li>
<li><strong>Witch hazel</strong> — despite marketing as "soothing," it is astringent and can worsen sensitivity</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Realistic Timeline</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">
<p><strong>Weeks 1–4:</strong> Baseline routine establishes comfort. Stinging and reactivity should reduce as barrier strengthens.</p>
<p><strong>Weeks 4–8:</strong> Skin should feel consistently calmer. Begin considering Tier 1 active introduction if desired.</p>
<p><strong>Months 3–6:</strong> Gradually expand routine complexity if tolerated. Some people find their optimal routine is the baseline — that is perfectly fine.</p>
<p><strong>Ongoing:</strong> Sensitive skin is often a permanent tendency, not a temporary phase. The goal is a stable, comfortable routine that you can maintain indefinitely.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="alert alert--caution" style="margin-top:var(--sp-8)">
<div class="alert__title">When to see a professional</div>
If your skin reacts to even the gentlest products (plain ceramide moisturisers, mineral sunscreen, water-only cleansing), you may have an underlying condition — contact dermatitis, rosacea, or eczema — that requires diagnosis. Persistent sensitivity that does not improve with a simplified routine warrants dermatological assessment, including possible patch testing for contact allergens.
