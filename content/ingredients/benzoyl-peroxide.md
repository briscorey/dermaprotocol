what_it_does: |
  Benzoyl peroxide is an oxidising agent that generates free radicals on contact with skin, which destroy *Cutibacterium acnes* bacteria (the primary bacterial contributor to inflammatory acne). It also has mild comedolytic (pore-unclogging) and anti-inflammatory properties.

  Its most important advantage over topical antibiotics (clindamycin, erythromycin) is that bacteria cannot develop resistance to its oxidising mechanism. For this reason, dermatological guidelines recommend combining benzoyl peroxide with any topical antibiotic to prevent resistance — and many dermatologists prefer benzoyl peroxide monotherapy for mild-to-moderate acne.

  Lower concentrations (2.5%) are as effective as higher concentrations (5–10%) for bacterial reduction, with significantly less irritation. This is one of the few cases where less is genuinely more.
best_use_cases:
  - "Inflammatory acne (papules, pustules)"
  - "Combined with topical antibiotics to prevent resistance"
  - "Short-contact therapy for sensitive skin (apply 5–10 min, then rinse)"
  - "Body acne (back, chest — wash formulations)"
  - "Maintenance therapy after acne clearance"
who_benefits: "Anyone with inflammatory acne, especially if avoiding or supplementing antibiotics. Particularly useful as a long-term maintenance treatment after initial clearance with prescription therapy."
caution_profile: "Common side effects: dryness, peeling, redness, and stinging — usually worst in weeks 1–2 and improving with continued use. Bleaches fabrics (towels, pillowcases, clothing). Allergic contact dermatitis is possible but uncommon (patch test if concerned). Increases sun sensitivity mildly. Use 2.5% concentration to minimise irritation with equivalent antibacterial efficacy."
common_mistakes:
  - "Using 10% concentration when 2.5% is equally effective with far less irritation"
  - "Applying to dry or compromised skin without buffering"
  - "Not warning about fabric bleaching (ruins pillowcases and towels)"
  - "Combining with retinoids in the same application step (can degrade retinoids)"
  - "Applying too much — a thin layer is sufficient"
  - "Neglecting short-contact therapy option for sensitive skin"
combines_well:
  - "Topical clindamycin (prevents antibiotic resistance — the standard combination)"
  - "Niacinamide (for oil control and barrier support)"
  - "Adapalene (prescription combination — FDA-approved fixed-dose product exists)"
combines_poorly:
  - "Retinol/tretinoin at the same time (BP may oxidise and degrade retinoids — split AM/PM)"
  - "Vitamin C at the same time (BP may oxidise L-ascorbic acid)"
  - "AHA/BHA exfoliants in the same step (irritation stacking)"
