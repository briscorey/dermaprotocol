<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Overview</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">
<p>Retinol and tretinoin are both forms of vitamin A that ultimately convert to the same active molecule (retinoic acid) in the skin. The difference is directness: tretinoin IS retinoic acid — no conversion required. Retinol needs two enzymatic conversions to become active. This makes tretinoin dramatically more potent, faster-acting, and more irritating.</p>
<p>This is not a question of which is "better" — it is a question of which is appropriate for your situation, tolerance, and access.</p>
</div>

<div class="detail-section" style="margin-top:var(--sp-10)">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Recommended Choice by Scenario</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">

<div class="alert alert--tip" style="margin-bottom:var(--sp-4)">
<div class="alert__title">If you have never used a retinoid before</div>
Start with retinol at 0.25–0.3%, 2–3 nights per week. Build tolerance over 8–12 weeks. This establishes whether your skin tolerates retinoids without the intensity of prescription tretinoin. Most people do very well here.
</div>

<div class="alert alert--tip" style="margin-bottom:var(--sp-4)">
<div class="alert__title">If retinol has been working well and you want stronger results</div>
This is the ideal time to discuss tretinoin with a dermatologist. Having retinol tolerance means your skin has adapted to the retinoid pathway, and the transition to tretinoin 0.025% will be much smoother than starting cold.
</div>

<div class="alert alert--tip" style="margin-bottom:var(--sp-4)">
<div class="alert__title">If your primary concern is acne (beyond mild)</div>
Tretinoin is the first-line prescription topical retinoid for acne. If OTC retinol is not adequately controlling your acne after 12 weeks, tretinoin is the evidence-based next step. Discuss with your dermatologist.
</div>

<div class="alert alert--tip" style="margin-bottom:var(--sp-4)">
<div class="alert__title">If you have sensitive or reactive skin</div>
Retinol with a buffer (apply moisturiser first, then retinol over it) at low frequency is the safest starting approach. Retinal (retinaldehyde) is an alternative that some sensitive-skinned people tolerate better than retinol.
</div>

<div class="alert alert--info">
<div class="alert__title">The supplement angle</div>
Regardless of which retinoid you choose, supporting your skin from the inside helps. <strong>Oral vitamin D</strong> supports the keratinocyte differentiation that retinoids stimulate. <strong>Omega-3s</strong> support barrier function during retinisation. <strong>Collagen peptides</strong> may complement the collagen-stimulating effects of retinoids.
