<div class="detail-section">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Overview</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">
<p>Retinal (retinaldehyde) and retinol are both over-the-counter forms of vitamin A that convert to retinoic acid in the skin. Retinal is one conversion step closer to the active form, which may make it faster-acting, though direct head-to-head evidence is more limited than the overall retinoid literature.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="detail-section" style="margin-top:var(--sp-10)">
<h2 class="detail-section__title">Recommended Choice by Scenario</h2>
<div class="detail-section__body">

<div class="alert alert--tip" style="margin-bottom:var(--sp-4)">
<div class="alert__title">If you are new to retinoids</div>
Start with retinol at a low concentration (0.25-0.3%). It has the most extensive evidence base, the widest product selection, and well-established usage guidelines.
</div>

<div class="alert alert--tip" style="margin-bottom:var(--sp-4)">
<div class="alert__title">If retinol irritates you but you want retinoid benefits</div>
Retinal may be worth trying. Some evidence suggests comparable efficacy with potentially less irritation, though individual responses vary.
</div>

<div class="alert alert--info">
<div class="alert__title">Keep expectations realistic</div>
Both are OTC retinoids. If you need stronger results, prescription tretinoin is the next step &mdash; but it requires professional guidance and has a more significant adjustment period.
</div>
